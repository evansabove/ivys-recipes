ingredients:
  - group: "For the cake"
    items:
      - "5 floz milk"
      - "5 floz vegetable oil"
      - "2 eggs"
      - "5oz caster sugar"
      - "7oz self-raising flour"
      - "2 tbsp cocoa powder"
      - "1 tsp baking powder"
      - "1 tsp bicarbonate of soda"
      - "1 tsp vanilla extract"
  - group: "For the icing"
    items:
      - "4oz butter (room temperature)"
      - "8oz icing sugar"
      - "(optional) drop of vanilla or peppermint extract"
intro: "This is the cake that started it all. She wouldn't just bake it for me: if I wanted cake, I had to be involved - and I was always glad to help! It set about my interest in baking - and eating - cakes. This is an oil-based cake, a little different from a traditional butter-based cake, and all the more moist for it."
steps:
  - "Preheat the oven to 150°C (fan)."
  - "Grease 2 x 20cm round cake tins, then drop a pinch of cocoa powder into each tin and tilt / shake it around to coat the bottom and sides. Tip out any excess."
  - "In a large mixing bowl, beat together the wet ingredients until combined: the milk, oil, eggs, sugar and vanilla."
  - "Set a sieve over the bowl and add the flour, cocoa powder, baking powder and bicarbonate of soda."
  - "Beat once more until combined (but no more, don't overwork the flour)."
  - "Pour immediately into the prepared tins and bake for around 30 minutes."
  - "Cool in the tins for a few minutes, then turn out onto a wire rack to cool completely."
  - "For the icing, beat the butter until soft, then beat in the icing sugar and vanilla/peppermint extract."
  - "Spread the icing over each side of the cake, then sandwich together."
serve: "Serve in generous slices. A cup of tea is non-negotiable."
tips:
  - "Do not ice this cake (or any cake, for that matter) until it is completely cold - you will regret it!"
  - "Try flavouring your icing - peppermint works well"
  - "A little food colouring in the icing can make this fun for kids (granny tried blue once, this is perhaps not my recommendation...)"
  - "Ice both sides of the cake, then sandwich them together - this prevents the icing from sticking to one side of the cake if it falls apart while being eaten. It's a professional touch!"
  - "A confession - I often do not sift the flour and cocoa, and it hasn't caused me a problem yet!"
